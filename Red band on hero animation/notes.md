# Editorial Portrait Eye-Band Reveal

## Primary Use Case

A cinematic portrait-intro animation built for key-character reveals, editorial posters, premium hero sections, title cards, and dark authority-driven opening frames. This pattern is especially useful when the portrait must feel dominant first, and then receive a secondary branded or emotional accent through a red eye-band sweep.

## Best Used For

- editorial poster intros
- character reveals
- authority-driven hero sections
- premium landing visuals
- cinematic creator intros
- dark title cards
- moody portrait features
- narrative visual openers
- anti-generic SaaS visual moments where a human subject must feel weighty and intentional

## Visual Behavior

The composition begins with a grayscale portrait entering with blur resolution, slight upward movement, and a controlled settle. Once the portrait has established itself, a strong red eye-band animates across the eye region as a dominant editorial overlay. The red treatment is not meant to feel like a background rectangle or generic highlight. It should feel attached to the portrait and tied to the emotional center of the face.

The final state holds with the portrait fully visible, the eye region charged with red emphasis, and the title locked near the lower region of the frame.

## Motion Sequence

1. The portrait enters from below with opacity increase, blur reduction, and scale correction.
2. The portrait lands with a small premium rebound.
3. A red fill establishes in the eye region.
4. A stronger red sweep passes across the eye line with skew and light bloom.
5. A flash layer enhances the sweep moment.
6. The title appears after the band action has completed.
7. Subtle ambient motion keeps the frame alive without distracting from the composition.

## Avoid Using When

- the portrait is very colorful and already visually busy
- the layout contains too many competing animated elements
- the tone is playful, friendly, or lightweight
- the asset is not face-dominant
- the design requires soft corporate friendliness rather than editorial force
- the face is angled so far away that the eye-band cannot read clearly
